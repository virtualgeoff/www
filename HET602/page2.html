2. The Motion of the Planets

Kepler's Laws

Johannes Kepler, using observational data of Tycho Brahe, published his laws of planetary motion in 1609 and 1618:

1st law:
"the orbit of a planet about the sun is an ellipse with the sun at one focus" (the law of orbits)
2nd law:
"a line joining a planet and the sun sweeps out equal areas in equal intervals of time" (the law of areas)
3rd law:
"the square of the sidereal period of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semimajor axis of the orbit" (the law of periods)
[Kaufmann and Freeman, pp.81-83]

Netwton's Law of Gravitation

Netwton's Law of Gravitation was published with his three laws of motion in 1687 in his Principia:

"two bodies attract each other with a force that is directly proportional to the mass of each body and inversely proportiaonal to the square of the distance between them."
[Kaufmann and Freeman, p.91]

Using these laws, Newton was able to derive Kepler's laws analytically. Kepler's first law is a consequence of the gravitational force being proportional to 1/r2. [see Meriam, pp.179-183 for a derivation]. Kepler's second law is true for any central forces (e.g. electrostatics, etc), and is a result of the conservation of angular momentum.

Newton also showed that the orbit of a bodies around the sun is not confined to ellipses; they can be also travel in (open) parabolas or hyperbolas.
